Boston Introduction

Boston, founded in the 17th century, has been the center of attention in New England since the colonial period. Boston was the place for spark of American Revolution. The key event in the growth of the American Revolution happened in Boston. The Boston Tea Party was a political protest by the Sons of Liberty on December 16, 1773 to protect British Parliament's unfair tax on tea. In result, chests of tea were throw into Boston Harbor.

As the "cradle of the Revolution", a trip to Boston is necessarily a trip into American history. In the 1770's, Boston was the center of the revolutionary movement. Faneuil Hall was a meeting place for revolutionary leader, but now it houses dozens of shops and restaurant. Many places from back then is now open for tours such as the Old State House, Paul Revere's house, and Old North Church. The Old State House was the site of the colonial government. The Old North Church and its famous lanterns are still in use. The site of the Boston Massacre where five colonists died has been preserved. The First Public School as known as Boston Latin School now was established in Boston. Some of its graduates include Samuel Adams, John Hancock, and Robert Treat Paine. Ben Franklin was one of its most notable dropouts. The Granary Burying Ground, Boston's third oldest cemetery, in the middle of town has the graves of patriots John Hancock, Paul Revere and the five victims of the Boston Massacre.

Boston Tea Party is commemorated in a floating ship museum, located at Congress Street in Boston, Massachusetts. Visitors don't just learn about history but reenact and relive them. Not far from the floating museum aboard the USS Constitution, America's first great warship. In addition to history, Boston offers wonderful cultural tradition. There are 23 major theatres, orchestras, and symphonies.

The Old Corner Bookstore, the oldest commercial building, was build in 1718 as an apothecary shop and home. Publisher William Ticknor and James Thomas Fields produced the works John Greenleaf Whittier, Louisa May Alcott, Henry Wadsworth Longfellow, Ralph Waldo Emerson, Harriet Beecher Stowe, Nathaniel Hawthorne, Charles Dickens, and Oliver Wendell Holmes, Sr. from this place. Many of whom were frequent visitor and became their favorite meeting place.

Many other attractions will be loved by kids. Boston Common, which once housed British troops, is now a beautiful park in the center of the city. The Franklin Park Zoo, a 72 acre zoo, is nearby, and other kid-friendly attractions. The Boston Science Museum offers over 400 hands-on exhibits. The New England Aquarium has more than 2000 sea creatures on display, including fish, turtle, shark, rays, whales, and penguins. Finally, the Boston Children's museum is known as a great place to educating and entertaining kids at the same time.

Millions of visitors a year come to Boston for its history. Once they arrive they discover that there is even more enjoyable activities in this amazing city.
