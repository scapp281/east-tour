New Haven Introduction

New Haven is the second largest city in Connecticut after Bridgeport. It is located on New Haven Harbor on the northern shore of the Long Island Sound, an estuary between Connecticut and Long Island, New York. From 1701 until 1873, the city served as a capital of Connecticut. New Haven had the first public tree planting program in America, producing a canopy of mature trees and large elm. That gave New Haven the nickname The Elm City. New Haven was founded in 1638 by English Puritans who left the Massachusetts Bay Colony under the leadership of the Reverend John Davenport and the London merchant Theophilus Eaton.

The city has had much to offer the leisure traveler. Several performing-arts centers and theaters, outstanding museums, and a growing number of notable restaurants. Much of what is worthwhile about New Haven can be credited to the presence of one of the world's most prestigious universities. Yale University is an integral part of the city's economy. Some of its notable alums are Noah Webster, Eli Whitney, Samuel Morse, and President William H Taft, George HW Bush, Bill Clinton, and George W Bush.

Little major history event has happened here, but there are some fun facts about this city. Yale awarded the first Doctor of Medicine degree in 1729 to a man who never practiced medicine. In 1806, Noah Webster published the first American dictionary. Eli Whitney invented the cotton gin, which is a key invention of the Industrial Revolution. Samuel Colt invented a revolver in 1836. John Todd, who was the first person in the world to request telephone service. Therefore, the first telephone switchboard was made here. The first hamburger was allegedly made and sold here.

Beinecke Rare Book & Manuscript Library was built in 1963. This architecture is the largest building the world designed for the preservation of rare manuscripts including, one of only 48 surviving Gutenberg Bibles and original manuscripts by Charles Dickens, Benjamin Franklin and Goethe.

Grove Street Cemetery is just three blocks north of the New Haven Green. This cemetery holds the graves of several famous people, including Charles Goodyear, Samuel Morse, Noah Webster and Eli Whitney. It was the first chartered cemetery in the country in 1797. Around the turn of the 19th century, Yale University's medical students would sneak behind the grand Egyptian Revival gate at night to dig up bodies for dissection.

Inside Yale University, America's third oldest university, an art gallery contains American masterworks by Winslow Homer, Edward Hopper and Jackson Pollock, as well as a European collection that includes Vincent van Gogh's The Night Cafe.
